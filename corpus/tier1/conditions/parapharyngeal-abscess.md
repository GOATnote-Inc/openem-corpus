# Parapharyngeal Abscess

## Recognition

**Definition:** Deep space neck infection involving the parapharyngeal (lateral pharyngeal) space — a fascially enclosed space extending from the skull base to the hyoid bone, lateral to the superior pharyngeal constrictor muscle and medial to the pterygoid muscles, parotid gland, and mandible. Divided by the styloid process into two clinically significant compartments:

- **Prestyloid compartment:** Contains fat, lymph nodes, internal maxillary artery, inferior alveolar nerve. Most infections originate here (dental, pharyngeal, tonsillar source).
- **Poststyloid compartment (carotid sheath):** Contains internal carotid artery, internal jugular vein, cranial nerves IX-XII, sympathetic chain. Infection here causes vascular complications (carotid erosion, IJV thrombosis) and cranial nerve palsies.

**Etiology:**
- Odontogenic (most common in adults — 30-50%): infected lower molars, periapical abscesses
- Pharyngeal/tonsillar: spread from peritonsillar abscess or pharyngitis
- Parotitis: direct extension from parotid space
- Iatrogenic: dental extraction, peritonsillar drainage, injection
- Penetrating trauma to neck or oral cavity
- IV drug use (injection into neck vessels)

**Microbiology:**
- Polymicrobial in 50-70%: Streptococcus (viridans group, GAS), anaerobes (Prevotella, Peptostreptococcus, Fusobacterium), Staphylococcus aureus (including MRSA)
- Fusobacterium necrophorum: Lemierre syndrome pathogen — IJV septic thrombophlebitis

**Clinical Features:**
- Fever, rigors, toxic appearance
- Unilateral neck swelling and induration below the angle of mandible
- Odynophagia, dysphagia
- Trismus (less severe than PTA — pterygoid inflammation is less direct)
- "Hot potato" voice or muffled speech
- Torticollis (head tilted toward affected side)
- Drooling (inability to manage secretions)

**Exam Findings:**
- External: unilateral neck swelling, tenderness, and induration posterior to the angle of mandible
- Intraoral: medial displacement of the lateral pharyngeal wall and tonsil (tonsil is pushed medially, not the peritonsillar tissue that bulges in PTA)
- Pharyngeal wall may appear smooth and bulging (distinct from peritonsillar bulge)
- Less trismus than PTA (but may be present)

**Red Flags for Vascular Complications (poststyloid involvement):**
- Cranial nerve deficits: hoarseness (CN X), tongue deviation (CN XII), Horner syndrome (sympathetic chain)
- Pulsatile neck mass (mycotic pseudoaneurysm of ICA)
- Sentinel bleed from ear, nose, or mouth (herald of carotid blowout)
- High spiking fevers with rigors (Lemierre syndrome — IJV septic thrombophlebitis)
- Ipsilateral neck cord tenderness along SCM

## Critical Actions

1. **Secure the airway first.** Parapharyngeal abscess can cause airway compromise from medial pharyngeal wall displacement and supraglottic edema. Assess for stridor, drooling, inability to lie flat. If intubation is needed, awake fiberoptic intubation is preferred — paralytic agents and blind orotracheal intubation risk complete airway obstruction. Have surgical airway equipment at bedside.
2. **Obtain CT neck with IV contrast** — the diagnostic study of choice. Identifies the abscess, delineates compartment involvement (prestyloid vs. poststyloid), assesses proximity to great vessels, and identifies extension to adjacent spaces (retropharyngeal, mediastinal).
3. **Start IV antibiotics immediately** after blood cultures. Do not delay for imaging.
4. **Consult ENT/oral surgery emergently** for surgical drainage. Most parapharyngeal abscesses require operative drainage under general anesthesia (external cervical approach or transoral approach depending on compartment).
5. **Obtain CT angiography if poststyloid involvement** is suspected (cranial nerve deficits, pulsatile mass, sentinel bleed) to evaluate carotid artery and IJV.
6. **Evaluate for Lemierre syndrome** — fever, rigors, neck tenderness along IJV, history of pharyngitis/tonsillitis followed by septic deterioration. CT with contrast shows IJV thrombosis. Blood cultures grow Fusobacterium necrophorum.
7. **Monitor for mediastinitis** — chest pain, widened mediastinum on CXR, septic shock. Deep neck infections spread to the mediastinum via the danger space (space 4), which extends from the skull base to the diaphragm.

## Differential Diagnosis

| Condition | Distinguishing Features |
|-----------|------------------------|
| Peritonsillar abscess | Peritonsillar bulging, uvula deviation, severe trismus; swelling is medial to superior constrictor; more common, easier to drain transorally |
| Retropharyngeal abscess | Midline posterior pharyngeal wall bulge, neck stiffness, refusal to flex neck; more common in children < 5; CT shows prevertebral collection |
| Ludwig angina | Bilateral submandibular induration, floor of mouth elevation, woody hard sublingual swelling; dental source; airway emergency from tongue displacement |
| Cervical lymphadenitis/abscess | Superficial neck mass, mobile, overlying erythema; no pharyngeal wall displacement; CT shows superficial collection anterior to SCM |
| Parotitis | Preauricular swelling, trismus, purulent discharge from Stensen duct on gland massage; no pharyngeal wall displacement |
| Infected branchial cleft cyst | Lateral neck mass along anterior border of SCM; may fluctuate; CT shows well-circumscribed cystic lesion |
| Carotid body tumor/paraganglioma | Painless lateral neck mass, splays carotid bifurcation on CT angiography, enhances briskly; no infection signs |
| Internal jugular vein thrombosis | Neck swelling along SCM, fever (if septic); CT shows non-enhancing IJV thrombus; may be complication of parapharyngeal infection (Lemierre) |

## Workup

**Labs:**
- CBC with differential — leukocytosis with left shift
- BMP — dehydration assessment, renal function for contrast
- CRP/ESR — elevated inflammatory markers (CRP often > 100 mg/L)
- Blood cultures x 2 — obtain before antibiotics; positive in 10-25%
- Lactate — if sepsis suspected
- Coagulation studies — DIC screening if septic
- Type and screen — if surgical drainage planned

**Imaging:**
- **CT neck with IV contrast (diagnostic standard):** ring-enhancing fluid collection in parapharyngeal space, fat stranding, identifies compartment (prestyloid vs. poststyloid), extension to adjacent spaces, relationship to great vessels. Sensitivity 90-95% for abscess vs. phlegmon.
- **CT angiography:** if poststyloid involvement — evaluates ICA pseudoaneurysm, IJV thrombosis
- **CXR:** assess for mediastinal widening (mediastinitis), pleural effusion (empyema from descending infection)
- **CT chest with contrast:** if mediastinitis suspected (chest pain, widened mediastinum, septic shock)
- **MRI neck:** superior soft tissue detail but rarely needed emergently; useful for skull base extension
- **Lateral soft tissue neck X-ray:** widened prevertebral soft tissue suggests retropharyngeal involvement (> 7 mm at C2, > 22 mm at C6 in adults)

**Bedside:**
- Flexible nasopharyngoscopy if available — assess supraglottic edema, vocal cord mobility, pharyngeal wall displacement. Do not perform if high suspicion for impending airway loss (go to OR instead).

## Treatment

**Airway Management:**
- Continuous monitoring, head of bed elevated
- Awake fiberoptic intubation is preferred if intubation required — maintains spontaneous ventilation and avoids laryngospasm/complete obstruction from paralysis
- Surgical cricothyrotomy equipment at bedside
- Avoid blind nasotracheal intubation (risk of abscess rupture and aspiration)
- If airway stable, proceed with imaging and antibiotics

**Antibiotics (start immediately, IV):**
- First-line: ampicillin-sulbactam 3 g IV q6h + metronidazole 500 mg IV q8h
- Alternative: clindamycin 900 mg IV q8h + ceftriaxone 2 g IV q24h
- MRSA coverage (if risk factors: IVDU, prior MRSA, nasal colonization): add vancomycin 15-20 mg/kg IV q8-12h (target trough 15-20 mcg/mL)
- Lemierre syndrome: high-dose metronidazole is essential for Fusobacterium coverage; some add heparin anticoagulation (controversial)
- Duration: IV antibiotics until clinical improvement (afebrile 48-72 hours, decreasing WBC, able to swallow), then transition to oral amoxicillin-clavulanate 875/125 mg PO BID to complete 14-21 day total course

**Surgical Drainage:**
- Definitive treatment for established abscess (phlegmon without drainable collection may respond to antibiotics alone in 10-15% of cases)
- External cervical approach (submandibular incision): standard for prestyloid and poststyloid compartments
- Transoral approach: may be used for small prestyloid abscesses if accessible and no vascular proximity concerns
- CT-guided aspiration: option for small, well-defined prestyloid abscesses in stable patients (lower success rate than operative drainage)
- Drains left in place until output < 10 mL/day

**Supportive Care:**
- IV fluid resuscitation (patients are volume-depleted from poor oral intake)
- Dexamethasone 10 mg IV q8h x 24-48 hours (reduces airway edema, controversial but widely used)
- Analgesics: acetaminophen 1000 mg IV q6h + ketorolac 15-30 mg IV q6h (avoid opioids if possible — respiratory depression risk in compromised airway)
- NPO until airway is secured and drainage is planned
- DVT prophylaxis if admitted (unless anticoagulated for Lemierre syndrome)

## Disposition

**All patients with parapharyngeal abscess require hospital admission.**

**ICU Admission:**
- Airway compromise or concern for impending obstruction
- Sepsis or septic shock
- Poststyloid compartment involvement (vascular structures at risk)
- Lemierre syndrome with septic emboli
- Mediastinal extension (descending mediastinitis)
- Carotid pseudoaneurysm or sentinel bleed
- Post-operative monitoring after surgical drainage

**Floor/Step-down:**
- Stable airway, prestyloid abscess without vascular proximity
- Adequate IV antibiotics initiated
- Planned for operative drainage or responding to antibiotics (CT-confirmed phlegmon without drainable collection)
- Serial examinations for clinical improvement

**Discharge criteria (after admission):**
- Afebrile for 48-72 hours
- WBC normalizing
- Tolerating oral intake and medications
- Drain removed (output < 10 mL/day)
- Transition to oral antibiotics to complete 14-21 day total course
- ENT follow-up within 1 week

## Pitfalls

1. **Misdiagnosing parapharyngeal abscess as peritonsillar abscess and attempting bedside drainage.** PTA causes peritonsillar bulging with uvula deviation; parapharyngeal abscess causes medial displacement of the entire lateral pharyngeal wall and tonsil. Needle aspiration of a parapharyngeal abscess risks inadequate drainage, carotid artery injury, or IJV laceration. CT imaging before any drainage attempt differentiates these two entities.

2. **Failing to evaluate the airway.** Parapharyngeal abscess causes progressive airway narrowing from medial pharyngeal wall displacement and supraglottic edema. Patients may decompensate rapidly. Assess for stridor, voice changes, drooling, and inability to lie flat. Have surgical airway equipment available.

3. **Delaying antibiotics while awaiting imaging.** Start IV antibiotics immediately after obtaining blood cultures. CT confirmation of abscess is important for surgical planning, but empiric treatment should not wait for radiology.

4. **Not obtaining CT angiography when poststyloid involvement is suspected.** Cranial nerve deficits (hoarseness, tongue deviation, Horner syndrome), pulsatile neck mass, or sentinel hemorrhage indicate involvement of the carotid sheath. CTA identifies pseudoaneurysm or IJV thrombosis that changes management. A standard CT with contrast may miss early vascular complications.

5. **Missing Lemierre syndrome.** High spiking fevers with rigors, neck tenderness along the SCM, and history of preceding pharyngitis or dental infection should prompt evaluation for IJV septic thrombophlebitis. Blood cultures for Fusobacterium necrophorum may take 48-72 hours to grow. CT with contrast shows IJV thrombosis. Septic pulmonary emboli cause multifocal cavitary lung lesions on chest CT.

6. **Not evaluating for mediastinal extension.** The danger space (space 4) communicates directly from the skull base to the posterior mediastinum. A parapharyngeal abscess that extends into this space can cause descending necrotizing mediastinitis — mortality 20-40%. Obtain CXR (widened mediastinum, pleural effusion) and CT chest with contrast if chest symptoms, progressive sepsis, or CT neck shows inferior extension below the hyoid.

7. **Using paralytic agents for intubation without considering the consequences.** Paralysis in a patient with a distorted supraglottic airway can convert a difficult airway into a cannot-intubate, cannot-ventilate situation. Awake fiberoptic intubation preserves muscle tone and spontaneous ventilation. If RSI is chosen, have a surgeon scrubbed and ready for surgical cricothyrotomy.

8. **Assuming antibiotics alone will resolve the infection.** While small phlegmons may respond to IV antibiotics, established abscesses with drainable collections require surgical drainage. CT-defined collections > 2.5 cm have low likelihood of resolution with antibiotics alone. Re-image at 48-72 hours if no clinical improvement on antibiotics.
