# Post-Tonsillectomy Hemorrhage

## Recognition

**Definition:** Bleeding from the tonsillar fossa following tonsillectomy. Classified as primary (within 24 hours of surgery, 0.5-2% incidence) or secondary (5-10 days postoperatively, 2-4% incidence). Secondary hemorrhage is more common and occurs as the eschar separates from the tonsillar fossa during wound healing. Overall post-tonsillectomy hemorrhage rate: 2-6%.

**Clinical Features:**
- Active bleeding from one or both tonsillar fossae — ranges from blood-tinged saliva to frank hemorrhage
- Blood in the oropharynx, hematemesis (from swallowed blood), or melena
- "Herald bleed" — a small, self-limited bleed that stops spontaneously, followed hours later by massive hemorrhage. This is the most dangerous presentation because patients may be discharged after the sentinel bleed.
- Frequent swallowing (occult posterior bleeding — the child swallows blood rather than spitting it out)
- Tachycardia, pallor, diaphoresis (signs of hypovolemia — may be late findings in children)

**Timing:**
- Primary (< 24 hours): typically intraoperative or in PACU, usually from inadequate hemostasis
- Secondary (days 5-10): eschar separation, infection of tonsillar bed, premature return to activity
- Peak secondary hemorrhage: postoperative day 7

**Pediatric Considerations:**
- Children compensate for blood loss longer than adults — tachycardia may be the only early sign
- Children swallow blood — hematemesis, bloody stools, or "coffee ground" emesis may be the presenting complaint, masking the oropharyngeal source
- Total blood volume in children is approximately 80 mL/kg — small absolute volumes represent significant percentage losses

## Critical Actions

1. **Secure the airway** — blood in the oropharynx risks aspiration. Position the patient upright and leaning forward. Have suction immediately available. If the patient cannot protect the airway, intubate (anticipate a difficult airway due to blood, edema, and distorted anatomy). Have two suction setups ready.
2. **Obtain IV access and send labs** — two large-bore IVs. Send CBC, coagulation studies (INR, PTT), type and crossmatch. Begin isotonic crystalloid resuscitation (20 mL/kg bolus in children, 1 L in adults).
3. **Direct visualization and initial hemostasis** — suction the oropharynx, identify the bleeding source. Apply direct pressure with gauze soaked in oxymetazoline 0.05% or 1:10,000 epinephrine to the tonsillar fossa.
4. **Silver nitrate cautery** — for small, visible bleeding vessels in cooperative patients.
5. **Notify ENT immediately** — all post-tonsillectomy hemorrhages require ENT consultation. Persistent bleeding requires return to the operating room for cautery, suture ligation, or re-exploration.
6. **Prepare for OR** — if bleeding does not stop with direct pressure and topical hemostatics within 15-20 minutes, the patient needs operative intervention. NPO status, prepare for general anesthesia.
7. **Transfuse early** — if hemoglobin < 7 g/dL or hemodynamic instability despite crystalloid resuscitation. In children, transfuse pRBC 10 mL/kg.

## Differential Diagnosis

| Condition | Distinguishing Features |
|-----------|------------------------|
| GI hemorrhage (upper) | No recent surgical history, epigastric pain, NSAID/alcohol use, liver stigmata; EGD shows esophageal/gastric source |
| Peritonsillar abscess | No recent tonsillectomy, progressive sore throat with trismus, peritonsillar bulge, uvular deviation |
| Posterior epistaxis | Bleeding from nasal cavity not tonsillar fossa, no surgical history, blood visible in anterior nares |
| Coagulopathy (systemic) | Bleeding from multiple sites, petechiae, ecchymoses, elevated INR/PTT, thrombocytopenia |
| Tonsillar vessel erosion (malignancy) | Chronic course, weight loss, tonsillar mass, no recent surgery — rare |
| Oropharyngeal laceration | Trauma history (e.g., child with object in mouth), visible mucosal tear distinct from tonsillar fossa |

## Workup

**Bedside:**
- Direct oropharyngeal inspection with headlamp, tongue depressor, and suction
- Quantify blood loss (ask about clots spit or swallowed, number of blood-soaked cloths)
- Assess hydration status: heart rate, blood pressure, capillary refill, mucous membranes
- In children: weigh blood-soaked gauze/towels (1 g = 1 mL blood loss)

**Labs:**
- CBC — hemoglobin and hematocrit (may not reflect acute blood loss for 6-12 hours)
- INR, PTT — rule out coagulopathy
- Type and crossmatch — all patients with active or recent post-tonsillectomy hemorrhage
- BMP — renal function, electrolytes (dehydration from poor oral intake postoperatively)

**Imaging:**
- Not routinely indicated
- CT neck with contrast — only if concern for deep space infection or pseudoaneurysm (rare, refractory hemorrhage)
- CT angiography — if concern for external carotid artery branch pseudoaneurysm in massive refractory hemorrhage

## Treatment

**Initial Hemostasis:**
- Direct pressure with gauze soaked in oxymetazoline 0.05% or 1:10,000 epinephrine — hold firmly against the tonsillar fossa for 10-15 minutes
- Silver nitrate cautery to identified bleeding vessel (cooperative patient, small vessel)
- Topical thrombin or tranexamic acid (TXA) soaked gauze applied to the bleeding fossa
- Tranexamic acid 1 g IV over 10 minutes (adults) or 15-20 mg/kg IV (children) — adjunctive for significant hemorrhage
- Hydrogen peroxide 3% gargle (breaks up clot to identify source — use only for visualization, not hemostasis)

**Operative (ENT):**
- Electrocautery of the bleeding vessel under general anesthesia
- Suture ligation of the bleeding vessel
- Re-exploration of the tonsillar fossa with debridement of eschar and definitive hemostasis
- Rarely: external carotid artery ligation for uncontrolled hemorrhage

**Resuscitation:**
- Isotonic crystalloid: 20 mL/kg bolus (children), 1 L bolus (adults), repeat as needed
- pRBC transfusion: 10 mL/kg (children), 1 unit (adults) — transfuse early in significant hemorrhage
- Correct coagulopathy: vitamin K, FFP, platelets, or PCC as indicated

**Pain Management:**
- Acetaminophen 15 mg/kg PO/IV (children), 1000 mg IV (adults)
- Avoid NSAIDs (ibuprofen, ketorolac) — impair platelet function and increase bleeding risk
- Opioids with caution: morphine 0.1 mg/kg IV (children), 4 mg IV (adults) — may depress airway reflexes

## Disposition

**Admit (all post-tonsillectomy hemorrhage patients):**
- Any active bleeding requiring intervention — admit for observation minimum 24 hours
- All patients requiring OR for hemostasis
- Herald bleed — even if bleeding has stopped. The patient must be admitted for observation because a herald bleed precedes massive hemorrhage in a significant percentage of cases
- Hemodynamic instability or need for transfusion — ICU admission
- Children with significant blood loss or hematemesis/melena

**Observation (minimum):**
- Self-limited bleed with no intervention needed — observe minimum 6 hours (some centers admit all)
- Ensure patient can tolerate oral fluids before considering discharge
- Close ENT follow-up within 24 hours

**Discharge (rare — only after extended observation):**
- Truly trivial blood-tinged saliva with no active bleeding on exam
- Normal vital signs, normal hemoglobin
- Reliable caregivers who can return immediately
- Strict return precautions: any further bleeding, hematemesis, melena, dizziness, or inability to swallow

## Pitfalls

1. **Dismissing a "herald bleed."** A small self-limited post-tonsillectomy bleed that stops on its own is not benign. It often precedes massive hemorrhage within hours. These patients require admission and ENT consultation, not discharge with reassurance.

2. **Underestimating blood loss in children.** Children maintain blood pressure until 25-40% of blood volume is lost. Tachycardia may be the only sign. A 20 kg child's total blood volume is 1600 mL — 400 mL of blood loss (a "small" amount) is 25% hemorrhage. Frequent swallowing can mask ongoing bleeding.

3. **Failing to examine the oropharynx.** The patient says "the bleeding stopped." Without direct inspection of the tonsillar fossae, you cannot confirm hemostasis. A clot sitting in the fossa may dislodge at any time.

4. **Not anticipating the difficult airway.** Intubation of a patient with active oropharyngeal hemorrhage is a high-risk procedure. Blood obscures landmarks, postoperative edema distorts anatomy, and the patient may have a full stomach from swallowed blood. Have two suction devices, a video laryngoscope, and surgical airway equipment immediately available.

5. **Prescribing NSAIDs for post-tonsillectomy pain.** Ibuprofen and ketorolac inhibit platelet aggregation and increase bleeding risk. Use acetaminophen as the primary analgesic. The AAO-HNS guideline supports ibuprofen in children post-tonsillectomy for routine pain, but in the setting of hemorrhage, avoid all NSAIDs.

6. **Attributing hematemesis or melena to a GI source without asking about recent surgery.** In children especially, swallowed blood from the tonsillar fossa presents as hematemesis or bloody/tarry stools. Always ask about recent ENT surgery in any child with upper GI bleeding.

7. **Delaying ENT consultation.** Post-tonsillectomy hemorrhage is a surgical emergency. ENT should be notified at triage. If the bleeding does not stop with direct pressure and topical agents within 15-20 minutes, the patient needs the OR — not more observation.
