# Corneal Ulcer (Infectious Keratitis)

## Recognition

**Presentation:**
- Eye pain (often severe), photophobia, tearing, foreign body sensation
- Decreased visual acuity
- Corneal opacity/infiltrate visible on penlight exam or slit lamp
- Mucopurulent discharge
- Conjunctival injection
- Possible hypopyon (indicates severe infection with secondary anterior chamber inflammation)

**Risk factors:**
- Contact lens wear (most common risk factor in developed countries) — especially overnight/extended wear
- Corneal trauma or abrasion
- Prior corneal surgery (LASIK, PRK)
- Dry eye, neurotrophic keratopathy
- Immunosuppression (topical steroids, systemic immunosuppression)
- Herpes simplex keratitis history

**Organisms by context:**
- Contact lens wearers: Pseudomonas aeruginosa (rapid, aggressive), Serratia marcescens
- Trauma: Staphylococcus aureus, Streptococcus, fungi (vegetative matter, soil)
- Post-LASIK: atypical mycobacteria
- Immunocompromised: fungi (Fusarium, Aspergillus), Acanthamoeba
- Acanthamoeba: severe pain out of proportion, ring infiltrate, contact lens use with tap water

## Critical Actions

| Action | Target |
|---|---|
| Contact lens removal | Immediate (send lens for culture) |
| Visual acuity | At presentation |
| Slit lamp exam | At presentation |
| Ophthalmology consultation | Same day for central/large ulcers |
| Start topical antibiotics | Within 1 hour |

1. **Remove contact lens** — save for culture if possible
2. **Visual acuity** — document baseline
3. **Slit lamp examination:** size and location of infiltrate/ulcer, depth of stromal involvement, anterior chamber reaction, hypopyon, corneal thinning/perforation
4. **Corneal scraping for culture** (ophthalmology performs for large, central, or severe ulcers)
5. **Start empiric topical antibiotics** — fluoroquinolone for most; fortified drops for severe
6. **Do NOT patch the eye** — traps bacteria and worsens infection
7. **Discontinue contact lens use** until fully resolved and cleared by ophthalmology

## Differential Diagnosis

- Corneal abrasion (no infiltrate, clear stroma, positive fluorescein defect only)
- Herpes simplex keratitis (dendritic ulcer on fluorescein staining, decreased corneal sensation)
- Herpes zoster ophthalmicus (dermatomal vesicular rash, pseudodendrite)
- Marginal keratitis (peripheral infiltrate near limbus, sterile — immune-mediated)
- Sterile corneal infiltrate (small, non-progressive, contact lens-related)
- Fungal keratitis (feathery margins, satellite lesions, slower course, plant/soil exposure)
- Acanthamoeba keratitis (ring infiltrate, severe pain, contact lens + water exposure)
- Anterior uveitis (no corneal infiltrate; cells and flare in anterior chamber)

## Workup

- **Slit lamp with fluorescein:** defines size, depth, location of ulcer; epithelial vs stromal involvement
- **Corneal scraping** (performed by ophthalmology): for Gram stain, culture (blood agar, chocolate agar, Sabouraud for fungi), and sensitivity
- **Contact lens and case culture** — if available
- **Confocal microscopy** — if Acanthamoeba suspected (not available in most EDs; ophthalmology referral)
- **Visual acuity:** Snellen chart
- **IOP:** may be elevated from inflammation or steroid use

## Treatment

### Mild-Moderate Ulcer (Peripheral, < 2 mm, No Hypopyon)
- **Moxifloxacin 0.5% ophthalmic** 1 drop to affected eye q1h while awake x 48 hours, then q2h while awake
- OR **gatifloxacin 0.5%** same dosing
- Ophthalmology follow-up in 24-48 hours

### Severe Ulcer (Central, > 2 mm, Hypopyon, Thinning)
- **Fortified tobramycin 14 mg/mL** 1 drop q1h alternating with
- **Fortified vancomycin 25 mg/mL** 1 drop q1h (30 minutes apart from tobramycin)
- These are compounded by the hospital pharmacy
- Alternative: fluoroquinolone q30min x 6 hours, then q1h if fortified drops not available

### Fungal Keratitis (Suspected or Confirmed)
- **Natamycin 5% ophthalmic suspension** 1 drop q1h while awake — first-line for filamentary fungi
- OR **voriconazole 1% ophthalmic** 1 drop q1h (yeast or if natamycin unavailable)
- **Oral voriconazole 200 mg PO BID** for deep or progressive fungal keratitis

### Acanthamoeba Keratitis
- **Polyhexamethylene biguanide (PHMB) 0.02%** 1 drop q1h alternating with
- **Propamidine 0.1% (Brolene)** 1 drop q1h
- Treatment duration: months; requires ophthalmology management

### Adjunctive
- **Cyclopentolate 1%** 1 drop TID — ciliary spasm relief, prevent synechiae
- **Oral analgesics:** acetaminophen 1000 mg PO q6h or ibuprofen 600 mg PO q6h
- **Do NOT prescribe topical steroids** from the ED — steroids worsen bacterial/fungal keratitis and must only be initiated by ophthalmology after culture results
- **Do NOT patch** the eye

## Disposition

- **Mild peripheral ulcer, good VA:** Discharge with fluoroquinolone drops q1h while awake; ophthalmology follow-up in 24 hours
- **Central ulcer, hypopyon, or threatened perforation:** Ophthalmology consultation in ED; admission if compliance uncertain or severe disease
- **Fungal or Acanthamoeba keratitis:** Ophthalmology referral for specialized management
- **Contact lens wearers:** discontinue lens use until cleared by ophthalmology; bring lens and case to ophthalmology appointment
- **All patients:** return immediately for vision deterioration, increasing pain, or discharge

## Pitfalls

1. **Prescribing topical steroids for a corneal ulcer.** Topical steroids dramatically worsen bacterial and fungal keratitis. Steroids may be used ONLY after culture results and under direct ophthalmology supervision. Never initiate from the ED.

2. **Missing Pseudomonas keratitis in a contact lens wearer.** Pseudomonas causes rapid corneal melting and perforation within 24-48 hours. Any contact lens wearer with a painful red eye and corneal infiltrate needs aggressive hourly fluoroquinolone drops and same-day ophthalmology follow-up.

3. **Patching the eye.** Eye patches create a warm, moist, dark environment that promotes bacterial growth. Never patch an infected cornea.

4. **Discharging without explicit instructions for hourly drop administration.** Bacterial keratitis requires drops every 1 hour while awake. Patients who misunderstand this as "four times a day" will have treatment failure. Written instructions are essential.

5. **Missing herpes simplex keratitis.** The dendritic ulcer pattern on fluorescein staining is pathognomonic. HSV keratitis requires antiviral therapy (ganciclovir gel or trifluridine drops); antibacterial drops alone will not treat the underlying virus and steroids will worsen it.
