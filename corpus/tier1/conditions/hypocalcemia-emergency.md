# Hypocalcemia Emergency

## Recognition

**Severity classification (ionized calcium):**
- Mild: iCa 3.2-4.0 mg/dL (0.8-1.0 mmol/L)
- Moderate: iCa 2.8-3.2 mg/dL (0.7-0.8 mmol/L)
- Severe: iCa < 2.8 mg/dL (< 0.7 mmol/L) or total Ca < 7.0 mg/dL

**Corrected calcium = measured Ca + 0.8 x (4.0 - albumin)**

**Neuromuscular manifestations:**
- Perioral and acral paresthesias (earliest symptom)
- Muscle cramps, carpopedal spasm
- Tetany (sustained muscle contraction)
- Laryngospasm (life-threatening — airway emergency)
- Seizures (generalized tonic-clonic)
- Chvostek sign: tapping facial nerve at angle of jaw causes ipsilateral facial muscle twitching (present in 10-25% of normal individuals)
- Trousseau sign: inflate BP cuff above SBP for 3 min → carpal spasm (more specific)

**Cardiovascular manifestations:**
- Prolonged QT interval → torsades de pointes
- Hypotension, impaired cardiac contractility
- Refractory heart failure
- Bradycardia

**Common etiologies:**
- Post-thyroidectomy/parathyroidectomy (most common acute cause; "hungry bone syndrome")
- Hypoparathyroidism (surgical, autoimmune, infiltrative)
- Vitamin D deficiency
- Hyperphosphatemia (renal failure — Ca x Phos product)
- Pancreatitis (calcium saponification)
- Massive blood transfusion (citrate chelation)
- Sepsis/critical illness
- Medications: bisphosphonates, denosumab, calcitonin, foscarnet, cisplatin

## Critical Actions

1. **IV calcium gluconate 10% — 1-2 grams (10-20 mL) in 50-100 mL D5W over 10-20 min** with continuous ECG monitoring
2. **Repeat bolus if symptoms persist** — may give up to 3 boluses
3. **Start continuous infusion:** calcium gluconate 10% — 100 mL (10 grams) in 1 L NS, run at 50-100 mL/hr (0.5-1 g/hr); titrate to symptoms and iCa
4. **Correct hypomagnesemia** — hypocalcemia is refractory without magnesium correction; magnesium sulfate 2 g IV over 15-30 min
5. **Check iCa, Mg, Phos, K q4-6h** during infusion
6. **Protect airway** if laryngospasm (have intubation equipment ready)

## Differential Diagnosis

- Hyperventilation with respiratory alkalosis (transient ionized hypocalcemia; correct with rebreathing)
- Hypomagnesemia (causes functional hypoparathyroidism; low Mg makes low Ca refractory)
- Hyperphosphatemia (renal failure; Ca x Phos precipitation)
- Pancreatitis (low Ca as prognostic marker; calcium saponification)
- Massive blood transfusion (citrate toxicity)
- Rhabdomyolysis (calcium deposits in necrotic muscle; releases later)
- Tumor lysis syndrome (hyperphosphatemia → hypocalcemia)
- Medication effect (bisphosphonates, denosumab, cisplatin)

## Workup

- **Ionized calcium** — most accurate; not affected by albumin or pH
- **Corrected total calcium** if ionized not immediately available
- **Magnesium** — must correct if low; hypocalcemia is refractory without normal Mg
- **Phosphorus** — elevated in renal failure, hypoparathyroidism; low in vitamin D deficiency
- **PTH (intact)** — low/absent = hypoparathyroidism; elevated = appropriate response (vitamin D deficiency, renal failure)
- **25-hydroxyvitamin D** — assess vitamin D stores
- **BMP:** creatinine, potassium (coexisting electrolyte abnormalities)
- **Albumin** — for calcium correction
- **ECG** — prolonged QTc; risk of torsades de pointes
- **Lipase** — if pancreatitis suspected
- **Recent surgical history** — post-thyroidectomy/parathyroidectomy

## Treatment

### Acute Symptomatic Hypocalcemia (Seizure, Tetany, Laryngospasm)
- **Calcium gluconate 10%** 1-2 grams IV (10-20 mL) in 50-100 mL D5W over 10-20 min
- Repeat q10-60 min until symptoms resolve
- ECG monitoring during bolus (rapid administration can cause bradycardia/arrest)
- **Calcium chloride 10%** 0.5-1 g IV (5-10 mL) — 3x more elemental calcium than gluconate; use ONLY via central line (severe tissue necrosis with peripheral extravasation)

### Continuous Infusion (After Bolus)
- **Calcium gluconate 10%** 100 mL (10 g) in 1 L NS or D5W — run at 50-100 mL/hr
- Titrate to maintain iCa > 4.0 mg/dL (1.0 mmol/L)
- Recheck iCa q4-6h
- Duration: typically 24-48h until oral calcium/vitamin D adequate

### Magnesium Correction (Mandatory)
- **Magnesium sulfate** 2 g IV over 15-30 min, then 1-2 g IV q6h PRN
- Target Mg > 2.0 mg/dL
- Hypocalcemia will NOT respond to calcium replacement if Mg is low (Mg required for PTH secretion and action)

### Oral Transition
- **Calcium carbonate** 1-2 g elemental calcium PO daily (divided TID with meals)
- **Calcitriol** 0.25-0.5 mcg PO BID — active vitamin D; bypasses need for renal 1-alpha-hydroxylation
- **Cholecalciferol** 2000-4000 IU PO daily — for vitamin D deficiency
- Begin oral supplementation early to facilitate IV calcium wean

### Hyperphosphatemia-Induced Hypocalcemia
- Correct phosphorus FIRST with phosphate binders (sevelamer 800-1600 mg PO TID with meals)
- IV calcium may worsen ectopic calcification if Ca x Phos product > 55
- Emergent dialysis if severe hyperphosphatemia with AKI

## Disposition

- **Symptomatic hypocalcemia (seizures, tetany, laryngospasm):** ICU with telemetry
- **Moderate hypocalcemia requiring IV infusion:** monitored bed with serial labs q4-6h
- **Post-thyroidectomy hypocalcemia, mild symptoms, responding to oral calcium:** may discharge with calcitriol 0.5 mcg PO BID + calcium 1500-2000 mg/day, follow-up in 24-48h
- **All patients:** endocrinology follow-up if new diagnosis of hypoparathyroidism

## Pitfalls

1. **Not correcting magnesium.** Hypomagnesemia causes PTH resistance and impaired PTH secretion. Calcium repletion will fail unless magnesium is normal. Always check and replace Mg.

2. **Giving calcium chloride via peripheral IV.** Calcium chloride causes severe tissue necrosis if extravasated. Use calcium gluconate for peripheral administration; reserve calcium chloride for central lines or code situations.

3. **Rapid IV calcium bolus without cardiac monitoring.** Rapid calcium administration causes bradycardia, heart block, and cardiac arrest. Infuse over 10-20 minutes with continuous ECG monitoring.

4. **Forgetting to check ionized calcium.** Total calcium is unreliable in hypoalbuminemia, acidosis, and alkalosis. Ionized calcium is the physiologically active form and should guide treatment.

5. **Not considering post-surgical hypoparathyroidism.** In any patient with recent neck surgery (thyroidectomy, parathyroidectomy) presenting with perioral tingling or muscle cramps, check calcium immediately.

6. **Replacing calcium in severe hyperphosphatemia without lowering phosphorus.** If Ca x Phos product > 55, calcium administration risks metastatic calcification. Reduce phosphorus first with binders or dialysis.
