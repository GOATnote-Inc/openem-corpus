# Epistaxis

## Recognition

**Definition:** Bleeding from the nasal cavity, classified as anterior (90%) or posterior (10%). Anterior bleeding originates from Kiesselbach plexus (Little area) on the anterior nasal septum — a vascular watershed of the anterior ethmoidal, sphenopalatine, superior labial, and greater palatine arteries. Posterior bleeding originates from branches of the sphenopalatine artery or posterior ethmoidal artery and can be life-threatening.

**Clinical Features:**
- Unilateral or bilateral nasal bleeding (anterior bleeds are almost always unilateral)
- Blood draining anteriorly from nares or posteriorly into oropharynx
- Posterior epistaxis: bleeding from both nares, blood pooling in posterior pharynx, hemoptysis, hematemesis from swallowed blood
- Hypertension (reactive vs causative — treat if severe)
- Signs of hemorrhagic shock in severe posterior bleeds: tachycardia, hypotension, pallor

**Risk Factors:**
- Anticoagulant/antiplatelet use (warfarin, DOACs, aspirin, clopidogrel)
- Hypertension
- Hereditary hemorrhagic telangiectasia (HHT/Osler-Weber-Rendu)
- Thrombocytopenia, platelet dysfunction, coagulopathies
- Nasal trauma, digital manipulation, nasal septal deviation
- Dry climate, low humidity
- Nasal cocaine use, intranasal corticosteroid sprays

## Critical Actions

1. **Assess hemodynamic stability** — posterior epistaxis can cause hemorrhagic shock. Obtain IV access and type/crossmatch in patients with significant blood loss or hemodynamic instability.
2. **Airway protection** — have the patient sit upright, lean forward, and spit blood into a basin. Posterior epistaxis with altered mental status may require intubation for airway protection.
3. **Direct pressure first** — pinch the soft cartilaginous nose (not the nasal bones) continuously for 15-20 minutes without releasing. This controls most anterior bleeds.
4. **Identify the source** — after clearing clot with suction and applying topical vasoconstrictor (oxymetazoline 0.05% spray), inspect the septum with a nasal speculum and headlamp. Anterior source = visible vessel on septum. No anterior source = assume posterior.
5. **Control bleeding** — anterior: silver nitrate cautery or anterior nasal packing. Posterior: posterior packing (Foley catheter, posterior epistaxis balloon) or Rapid Rhino device.
6. **Reverse anticoagulation** if clinically significant hemorrhage: warfarin → vitamin K 10 mg IV + 4-factor PCC; DOACs → idarucizumab (dabigatran) or andexanet alfa (factor Xa inhibitors) if available.
7. **Labs** — CBC, coagulation studies (INR, PTT), type and screen in posterior or severe bleeds.

## Differential Diagnosis

| Condition | Distinguishing Features |
|-----------|------------------------|
| Le Fort fracture | History of significant facial trauma, midface mobility, step-offs at buttresses, malocclusion, CSF rhinorrhea |
| Nasal foreign body | Unilateral foul-smelling discharge, more common in children, visible foreign body on exam |
| Nasopharyngeal angiofibroma | Adolescent males, recurrent unilateral epistaxis, nasal obstruction, CT/MRI shows enhancing mass |
| Coagulopathy (systemic) | Bilateral/recurrent epistaxis, concurrent bleeding at other mucosal sites, petechiae, medication history |
| Hereditary hemorrhagic telangiectasia | Recurrent epistaxis, visible mucocutaneous telangiectasias (lips, tongue, fingers), family history, iron deficiency anemia |
| Nasal cavity tumor (SCC, melanoma) | Unilateral obstruction, epistaxis, facial pain, cranial nerve deficits in advanced disease |
| Posterior epistaxis vs hemoptysis | Posterior epistaxis: blood in nasopharynx, no cough prodrome; hemoptysis: blood with cough, frothy sputum |

## Workup

**Bedside:**
- Direct anterior rhinoscopy with nasal speculum and headlamp after applying topical vasoconstrictor
- Suction to clear clot and identify bleeding source
- Blood pressure monitoring — treat severe hypertension (SBP > 180) with IV labetalol or nicardipine

**Labs:**
- CBC — baseline hemoglobin, platelet count
- INR, PTT — if on anticoagulants or suspected coagulopathy
- Type and screen/crossmatch — posterior or hemodynamically significant bleeds
- BMP — baseline renal function

**Imaging:**
- Not routinely indicated for straightforward epistaxis
- CT face with contrast — if suspected posterior source refractory to packing, nasal mass, or concern for Le Fort fracture
- CT angiography — for refractory posterior epistaxis being considered for embolization

## Treatment

**Anterior Epistaxis:**

*First-line:*
- Direct pressure 15-20 minutes (pinch cartilaginous nose)
- Topical vasoconstrictor: oxymetazoline 0.05% spray (2 sprays each nostril)
- Topical anesthesia: 4% lidocaine with 1:100,000 epinephrine on cotton pledgets x 10 minutes

*Chemical cautery:*
- Silver nitrate application to the identified bleeding vessel for 5-10 seconds
- Apply in a radial pattern around the vessel, then directly to the vessel
- Cauterize only one side of the septum per visit (bilateral cautery risks septal perforation)

*Anterior packing (if cautery fails):*
- Merocel (compressed sponge): insert along nasal floor, hydrate with saline — expands to tamponade
- Rapid Rhino (inflatable balloon with carboxymethylcellulose coating): soak in water, insert, inflate with air
- Ribbon gauze packing with petroleum jelly (traditional method): layer from floor to roof of nasal cavity
- All anterior packs: leave 48-72 hours, prescribe antibiotics (amoxicillin-clavulanate 875/125 mg PO BID or cephalexin 500 mg PO QID) to prevent sinusitis and toxic shock syndrome

**Posterior Epistaxis:**

- Posterior epistaxis balloon (dual-balloon device): insert, inflate posterior balloon with 7-10 mL saline, inflate anterior balloon with 15-30 mL saline, apply anterior traction
- Foley catheter method: insert 12-14 Fr Foley through the nose into the nasopharynx, inflate balloon with 10-15 mL saline, retract anteriorly to tamponade the posterior choana, secure with an umbilical clamp at the naris (pad with gauze to prevent alar necrosis), place anterior pack
- Posterior packing is painful — provide adequate analgesia (morphine 4 mg IV or fentanyl 50-100 mcg IV)

**Refractory bleeding:**
- Interventional radiology: sphenopalatine artery embolization (success rate > 90%)
- Surgical: endoscopic sphenopalatine artery ligation (ENT)

## Disposition

**Discharge (anterior epistaxis):**
- Bleeding controlled with cautery alone or anterior packing
- Hemodynamically stable, normal hemoglobin
- Discharge instructions: no nose blowing, no heavy lifting x 48 hours, saline nasal spray, humidifier
- Follow-up with ENT or PCP in 48-72 hours for pack removal (if packed)
- If on anticoagulants: coordinate with prescribing physician regarding anticoagulation management

**Admit (posterior epistaxis or significant anterior):**
- All posterior nasal packing requires admission (risk of hypoxia, vagal response, aspiration)
- ICU or step-down monitoring for posterior packs (risk of airway obstruction if pack dislodges)
- Hemodynamically unstable patients requiring resuscitation
- Patients requiring anticoagulation reversal
- Failed anterior packing with ongoing hemorrhage
- Significant anemia requiring transfusion

**ENT Consultation:**
- Posterior epistaxis — urgent ENT consultation
- Anterior epistaxis refractory to cautery and packing
- Recurrent epistaxis (>= 3 episodes) — outpatient ENT referral for nasal endoscopy
- Suspected HHT — genetics and ENT referral

## Pitfalls

1. **Assuming all epistaxis is anterior.** Blood draining posteriorly into the pharynx, bilateral nasal bleeding, or failure to identify an anterior source on rhinoscopy should prompt posterior epistaxis management. Posterior epistaxis has significant morbidity and mortality.

2. **Releasing pressure too early or incorrectly.** Pinching the nasal bones does nothing — the cartilaginous soft nose must be compressed. Pressure must be continuous for 15-20 minutes without checking. Premature release disrupts clot formation.

3. **Failing to reverse anticoagulation in life-threatening hemorrhage.** Patients on warfarin with active posterior epistaxis need vitamin K 10 mg IV plus 4-factor PCC (not FFP — PCC reverses faster). DOAC reversal agents should be used when available for hemodynamically significant bleeds.

4. **Sending a posterior-packed patient to the floor without monitoring.** Posterior packs can dislodge and obstruct the airway. Patients require continuous pulse oximetry and cardiac monitoring. Supplemental oxygen should be administered.

5. **Not considering systemic causes of epistaxis.** Recurrent or severe epistaxis may be the presenting sign of leukemia, thrombocytopenia, von Willebrand disease, or HHT. Check CBC, coagulation studies, and examine for telangiectasias and petechiae.

6. **Cauterizing both sides of the septum.** Bilateral septal cautery at the same visit risks septal perforation from ischemic necrosis. If both sides are bleeding, cauterize one side and pack the other.

7. **Neglecting to prescribe prophylactic antibiotics with nasal packing.** Nasal packs create a warm, moist, obstructed environment — risk of sinusitis and staphylococcal toxic shock syndrome. Cover with oral antibiotics for the duration of packing.
