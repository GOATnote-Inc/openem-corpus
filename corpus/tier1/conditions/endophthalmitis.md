# Endophthalmitis

## Recognition

**Post-operative endophthalmitis (most common):**
- Typically 2-7 days after intraocular surgery (cataract surgery most frequent)
- Progressive pain, decreased vision, eyelid swelling
- Hypopyon (layered white cells in anterior chamber)
- Conjunctival injection
- Vitreous haze or opacity on fundoscopy
- Organisms: coagulase-negative Staphylococcus (most common), Staphylococcus aureus, Streptococcus, Pseudomonas, fungi

**Post-traumatic endophthalmitis:**
- After penetrating globe injury or intraocular foreign body
- Onset 24-72 hours post-injury
- High virulence: Bacillus cereus (devastating — rapid progression to no light perception)
- Staphylococcus, Streptococcus, fungi (soil contamination)

**Endogenous (hematogenous) endophthalmitis:**
- Metastatic seeding from bacteremia/fungemia
- IV drug users, immunocompromised, indwelling catheters, endocarditis
- Candida (most common fungal), Klebsiella (most common bacterial in East Asia), Staphylococcus aureus
- Bilateral in 25% of cases

**Key clinical features:**
- Acute onset decreased visual acuity (light perception or worse)
- Pain (may be absent in chronic or fungal forms)
- Hypopyon (visible on penlight exam)
- Loss of red reflex
- Relative afferent pupillary defect (if severe)

## Critical Actions

| Action | Target |
|---|---|
| Ophthalmology consultation | IMMEDIATE — this is an ophthalmologic emergency |
| Intravitreal antibiotic injection | < 6 hours |
| Vitreous and aqueous tap for culture | At time of injection |
| Do NOT give topical antibiotics alone | Critical — intravitreal route required |

1. **Emergent ophthalmology consultation** — this is one of the most time-sensitive ophthalmologic emergencies
2. **Visual acuity assessment** — document; prognostic importance (hand motion or worse = poor prognosis)
3. **Penlight exam:** hypopyon, conjunctival injection, corneal clarity
4. **B-scan ultrasound** if posterior segment not visible (vitreous opacities, retinal detachment)
5. **Do NOT start systemic antibiotics alone** — intravitreal injection is the primary therapy
6. **Blood cultures** if endogenous endophthalmitis suspected

## Differential Diagnosis

- Severe anterior uveitis/iritis (hypopyon can occur; typically less vitreous involvement)
- Sterile post-operative inflammation (TASS — toxic anterior segment syndrome; onset < 24 hours, less pain)
- Retained lens fragment (post-cataract surgery, gradual inflammation)
- Sympathetic ophthalmia (bilateral granulomatous uveitis after penetrating trauma — weeks to months)
- Retinal detachment (painless vision loss, floaters, curtain)
- Vitreous hemorrhage (painless, sudden vision loss, DM history)
- Intraocular tumor (lymphoma, retinoblastoma — slower course)
- Phacoanaphylactic uveitis (lens protein reaction)

## Workup

- **Visual acuity:** quantitative — Snellen chart; if unable, check hand motion, light perception, no light perception
- **Slit lamp examination:** anterior chamber reaction, hypopyon, corneal clarity, lens status, wound integrity (if post-operative)
- **B-scan ultrasonography:** vitreous opacities/debris, retinal detachment, choroidal thickening
- **Intraocular fluid sampling (performed by ophthalmology):** vitreous tap (0.1-0.2 mL) and/or anterior chamber tap (0.1 mL); Gram stain, culture, sensitivity
- **Blood cultures x 2:** if endogenous endophthalmitis suspected
- **Echocardiography:** if endocarditis as source suspected
- **CBC, CRP, procalcitonin:** systemic infection markers
- **Urine culture, fundal blood culture:** if fungal endogenous suspected

## Treatment

### Intravitreal Antibiotics (EMERGENT — performed by ophthalmology)
- **Vancomycin 1 mg/0.1 mL intravitreal injection** (gram-positive coverage)
- PLUS **ceftazidime 2.25 mg/0.1 mL intravitreal injection** (gram-negative coverage)
- OR **amikacin 0.4 mg/0.1 mL intravitreal injection** (alternative gram-negative; higher retinal toxicity risk)

### Post-Traumatic (Bacillus Risk)
- **Vancomycin 1 mg/0.1 mL intravitreal** PLUS **ceftazidime 2.25 mg/0.1 mL intravitreal**
- ADD **clindamycin 1 mg/0.1 mL intravitreal** — Bacillus coverage
- **Systemic:** vancomycin 15-20 mg/kg IV q8-12h + ceftazidime 2 g IV q8h (supplements intravitreal for penetrating trauma)

### Fungal Endophthalmitis
- **Voriconazole 100 mcg/0.1 mL intravitreal injection**
- OR **amphotericin B 5-10 mcg/0.1 mL intravitreal injection**
- **Systemic:** voriconazole 6 mg/kg IV q12h x 2 doses then 4 mg/kg IV q12h; or fluconazole 400 mg IV/PO daily

### Vitrectomy (Pars Plana Vitrectomy)
- Per EVS: recommended when visual acuity is light perception or worse
- Debulks infectious material, improves antibiotic distribution
- Emergent vitrectomy for Bacillus-suspected cases regardless of VA

### Topical Adjuncts
- **Topical fortified vancomycin 25-50 mg/mL** 1 drop q1h while awake
- **Topical fortified ceftazidime 50 mg/mL** 1 drop q1h while awake
- **Cyclopentolate 1%** 1 drop TID (cycloplegia for pain relief, prevent synechiae)
- **Prednisolone acetate 1%** 1 drop q1-2h (controversial — some advocate intravitreal dexamethasone 0.4 mg instead)

### Systemic Antibiotics
- **Post-operative endophthalmitis:** systemic antibiotics are adjunctive, not primary (per EVS, systemic did not improve outcomes)
- **Post-traumatic or endogenous:** systemic antibiotics ARE indicated (treat the source)
- **Endogenous:** treat underlying source (remove infected catheter, treat endocarditis, etc.)

## Disposition

- **All endophthalmitis:** Admission or close outpatient follow-up under ophthalmology (within 24 hours)
- **Post-operative, after intravitreal injection:** some institutions manage as urgent outpatient with daily ophthalmology exams
- **Post-traumatic:** Admission; may need surgical repair + intravitreal antibiotics
- **Endogenous:** Admission for IV antibiotics and source control (ID consultation)
- **Serial visual acuity monitoring** — worsening vision may require repeat intravitreal injection or vitrectomy

## Pitfalls

1. **Treating with topical or systemic antibiotics alone without intravitreal injection.** Topical and systemic antibiotics do not achieve adequate intraocular concentrations. Intravitreal injection is the primary therapy and must not be delayed.

2. **Dismissing post-cataract surgery eye pain as "normal healing."** Post-operative endophthalmitis presents 2-7 days after surgery with increasing pain and decreasing vision. Any post-surgical patient reporting new or worsening symptoms needs same-day ophthalmology evaluation.

3. **Not considering endogenous endophthalmitis in a febrile patient with new vision loss.** Bacteremia and fungemia can seed the eye. IV drug users, ICU patients, and immunocompromised individuals with acute unilateral vision loss and pain need dilated fundoscopy.

4. **Missing Bacillus cereus in post-traumatic endophthalmitis.** Bacillus endophthalmitis progresses to no light perception within 24-48 hours. Any penetrating globe injury with soil contamination needs emergent intravitreal antibiotics including clindamycin.

5. **Delaying ophthalmology consultation while obtaining cultures.** Vitreous and aqueous cultures are obtained at the time of intravitreal injection by the ophthalmologist. The ED role is to recognize the diagnosis and get ophthalmology there urgently.
