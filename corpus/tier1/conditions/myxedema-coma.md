# Myxedema Coma

## Recognition

**Clinical presentation:**
- Altered mental status progressing to coma (encephalopathy is defining feature)
- Hypothermia (core temp < 35C; may be profound < 30C; standard thermometers may not register)
- Hypotension and bradycardia (cardiovascular collapse)
- Hypoventilation with hypoxia and hypercapnia (respiratory failure)
- Non-pitting edema (myxedema), periorbital edema, macroglossia
- Delayed deep tendon reflexes (prolonged relaxation phase)
- Ileus, constipation, abdominal distension
- Hyponatremia (from impaired free water excretion)

**Precipitating factors (usually a stressor in a patient with untreated/undertreated hypothyroidism):**
- Infection (pneumonia, UTI — most common trigger)
- Cold exposure
- Medication non-adherence (levothyroxine discontinuation)
- Opioids, sedatives, anesthesia
- Stroke, MI, GI bleeding, trauma
- Surgery

**Key diagnostic features:**
- Elevated TSH (primary) or low/normal TSH (central/secondary hypothyroidism)
- Very low free T4 and free T3
- History of hypothyroidism, thyroidectomy, radioiodine ablation, or pituitary disease
- Mortality: 25-60% even with treatment

## Critical Actions

1. **Secure airway** — intubate for respiratory failure or GCS < 8; myxedema coma patients have high risk of aspiration
2. **IV levothyroxine (T4) 200-400 mcg IV bolus** — loading dose; oral route unreliable due to ileus and impaired absorption
3. **IV hydrocortisone 100 mg IV bolus** BEFORE or concurrent with thyroid hormone — to prevent adrenal crisis (coexisting adrenal insufficiency must be assumed until excluded)
4. **Passive rewarming** — warm blankets, warmed IV fluids; avoid active external rewarming (causes vasodilation and cardiovascular collapse)
5. **Treat hypotension** — IV NS bolus; vasopressors (norepinephrine 0.05-0.3 mcg/kg/min) if refractory; avoid fluid overload
6. **Search for and treat precipitant** — blood cultures, CXR, UA; empiric antibiotics if infection suspected

## Differential Diagnosis

- Sepsis (fever usually present; though myxedema can mask fever)
- Adrenal crisis (similar hypotension; check cortisol; often coexists)
- Hypothermia from exposure (no thyroid history, normal TSH)
- Drug overdose (opioids, benzodiazepines, barbiturates — check toxicology)
- Hepatic encephalopathy (elevated ammonia, liver disease)
- Uremic encephalopathy (elevated BUN/creatinine)
- Severe heart failure (elevated BNP, pulmonary edema)
- Carbon dioxide narcosis (COPD, hypoventilation)

## Workup

- **TSH, free T4, free T3** — send STAT; do NOT wait for results before treating if clinical suspicion is high
- **Cortisol level** — obtain before giving hydrocortisone; random cortisol < 18 mcg/dL suggests adrenal insufficiency
- **BMP:** hyponatremia (common), hypoglycemia, elevated creatinine
- **CBC:** anemia (common), leukocytosis if infection
- **ABG:** hypoxia, hypercapnia, respiratory acidosis
- **Lactate** — assess tissue perfusion
- **CK** — elevated from myopathy
- **Lipid panel** — markedly elevated cholesterol (supports severe hypothyroidism)
- **ECG:** sinus bradycardia, low voltage, prolonged QT, J waves (Osborn waves if hypothermic)
- **CXR:** pericardial effusion, cardiomegaly, pleural effusions, pneumonia
- **Core temperature** — use hypothermia-capable thermometer (rectal or esophageal)
- **Blood cultures, UA, procalcitonin** — infection screen

## Treatment

### Thyroid Hormone Replacement
- **IV levothyroxine (T4)** 200-400 mcg IV bolus on day 1, then 50-100 mcg IV daily
- **Optional T3 addition:** liothyronine 5-20 mcg IV q8h — faster onset than T4; controversial; consider in severe cases or if no improvement on T4 alone after 24-48h
- Transition to oral levothyroxine once patient can tolerate PO (1.6 mcg/kg/day)
- Reduce T4 loading dose in elderly, coronary artery disease, or arrhythmias (200 mcg initial)

### Stress-Dose Steroids
- **Hydrocortisone 100 mg IV bolus, then 50 mg IV q8h** until adrenal insufficiency excluded (cortisol result)
- Taper rapidly once cortisol confirmed adequate and patient improving

### Hypothermia Management
- Passive rewarming: warm blankets, warm environment (room temp 25C)
- Warmed IV fluids (40C)
- Do NOT use active external rewarming (heating pads, forced air) — causes peripheral vasodilation and cardiovascular collapse
- Bear Hugger at low setting only if core temp < 30C and no response to passive measures

### Hyponatremia
- Correct with fluid restriction and thyroid hormone replacement (corrects impaired free water excretion)
- Hypertonic saline (3% NaCl) only if Na < 120 mEq/L with seizures or severe symptoms
- Correction rate: < 8-10 mEq/L in 24 hours to avoid osmotic demyelination

### Hemodynamic Support
- IV NS bolus 250-500 mL — cautious (impaired cardiac function)
- Norepinephrine 0.05-0.3 mcg/kg/min IV if hypotension refractory to fluids and thyroid hormone
- Vasopressors may be poorly responsive until thyroid hormone takes effect (12-24h)

## Disposition

- **All myxedema coma:** ICU admission with continuous monitoring
- **Intubated patients:** mechanical ventilation in ICU
- **Transfer:** any ED without ICU or IV thyroid hormone availability must transfer immediately
- **Duration:** ICU until hemodynamically stable, off vasopressors, tolerating oral medications

## Pitfalls

1. **Waiting for lab results before starting treatment.** Myxedema coma is a clinical diagnosis. TSH and T4 results may take hours. If clinical suspicion is high, administer IV levothyroxine and hydrocortisone immediately.

2. **Not giving stress-dose steroids.** Up to 10% of myxedema coma patients have coexisting adrenal insufficiency (Schmidt syndrome/polyglandular autoimmune syndrome). Thyroid hormone replacement without cortisol replacement can precipitate adrenal crisis and death.

3. **Aggressive active rewarming.** Active external rewarming (heating blankets, forced air) causes peripheral vasodilation in a patient with marginal cardiac output, leading to cardiovascular collapse. Use passive rewarming only.

4. **Using oral levothyroxine.** Ileus, impaired GI absorption, and impaired consciousness make oral administration unreliable. IV levothyroxine is mandatory for the initial loading dose.

5. **Missing the precipitant.** Myxedema coma does not occur spontaneously — there is always a trigger. Failure to identify and treat the precipitating infection, medication change, or physiologic stressor leads to treatment failure.

6. **Standard thermometers missing severe hypothermia.** Core temperature in myxedema coma can be < 30C. Use a hypothermia-range rectal or esophageal thermometer. Standard oral thermometers may read "low" without providing the actual temperature.
