# Nasal Septal Hematoma

## Recognition

**Definition:** Collection of blood between the septal cartilage (or bone) and its overlying mucoperichondrium/mucoperiosteum. Occurs after nasal trauma — the subperichondrial blood dissects the perichondrium off the cartilage, depriving the avascular cartilage of its sole blood supply. If untreated, avascular necrosis of the septal cartilage occurs within 48-72 hours, leading to septal perforation, saddle nose deformity, or septal abscess.

**Epidemiology:**
- Occurs in 0.8-1.6% of nasal fractures
- More common in children (thicker, more vascular mucoperichondrium; more compliant cartilage that buckles rather than fractures)
- Often bilateral (blood dissects across cartilage from one side to the other)

**Clinical Features:**
- Nasal obstruction (bilateral, out of proportion to external injury)
- Septal pain and pressure sensation
- Rhinorrhea (may be blood-tinged)
- History of nasal trauma (may be minor — even a ball strike or fall)

**Exam Findings:**
- Bilateral boggy, bluish-purple, grape-like swelling on the nasal septum
- Fluctuant mass on palpation with cotton-tipped applicator
- Swelling does NOT shrink after application of topical oxymetazoline (distinguishes from turbinate engorgement or mucosal edema)
- May be bilateral — always examine both sides of the septum
- Overlying mucosa intact (unless lacerated by trauma)

**Red Flags for Abscess (late presentation):**
- Fever
- Erythema and increased tenderness
- Purulent nasal discharge
- Septal fluctuance with surrounding induration
- Presentation > 72 hours after trauma without prior drainage

## Critical Actions

1. **Examine the nasal septum in every patient with nasal trauma.** This is the single most important action. Septal hematoma is a clinical diagnosis made by anterior rhinoscopy. A headlamp and nasal speculum are required. Failure to look is the most common reason for missed diagnosis.
2. **Apply topical decongestant (oxymetazoline 0.05%) to differentiate septal hematoma from turbinate or mucosal edema.** Turbinate engorgement shrinks with decongestant; septal hematoma does not.
3. **Drain the hematoma emergently.** Incision and aspiration should be performed in the ED. Do not defer to outpatient ENT.
4. **Pack the nose or place quilting sutures after drainage** to prevent reaccumulation.
5. **Prescribe prophylactic antibiotics** to prevent septal abscess: amoxicillin-clavulanate 875/125 mg PO BID x 7 days OR cephalexin 500 mg PO QID x 7 days.
6. **Arrange follow-up within 24-48 hours** — reaccumulation occurs in 10-15% of cases.
7. **Treat the underlying nasal fracture** after addressing the septal hematoma. Nasal fracture reduction can wait 5-7 days for swelling to resolve; septal hematoma cannot wait.

## Differential Diagnosis

| Condition | Distinguishing Features |
|-----------|------------------------|
| Turbinate hypertrophy/mucosal edema | Shrinks with topical oxymetazoline; lateral nasal wall swelling, not septal; non-fluctuant |
| Nasal fracture (without hematoma) | Bony crepitus, deviation, periorbital ecchymosis; septum is flat without boggy swelling |
| Septal deviation (pre-existing) | Firm, non-fluctuant, non-tender cartilaginous or bony deviation; chronic history |
| Nasal foreign body | Unilateral foul-smelling discharge (especially in children); foreign body visible on rhinoscopy |
| Septal abscess | Late complication of untreated septal hematoma; fever, erythema, purulent material on aspiration; may have cartilage destruction already |
| Nasal polyp | Pale, pedunculated, non-tender, non-fluctuant mass; typically bilateral; chronic history |
| Rhinosinusitis | Mucosal edema, purulent discharge, facial pain/pressure; no discrete septal mass |

## Workup

**Bedside (diagnosis is clinical):**
- Anterior rhinoscopy with nasal speculum and headlamp
- Palpation of septum with cotton-tipped applicator (fluctuance confirms hematoma)
- Topical decongestant test (oxymetazoline 0.05% spray — hematoma will not shrink)
- Aspirate with 18-gauge needle if diagnosis uncertain — blood confirms hematoma

**Imaging:**
- Not required for diagnosis of septal hematoma
- CT face without contrast — if concern for associated nasal/midface fractures
- CT with contrast — if concern for septal abscess (rim-enhancing fluid collection with surrounding soft tissue inflammation)

**Labs:**
- Not routinely required
- CBC, CRP — if septal abscess suspected (leukocytosis, elevated inflammatory markers)
- Coagulation studies (PT/INR, PTT) — if patient on anticoagulants or has a coagulopathy (predisposes to hematoma formation after minimal trauma)

## Treatment

**Incision and Drainage (performed in ED):**
1. Position patient upright in sniffing position
2. Topical anesthesia: oxymetazoline 0.05% spray + 4% lidocaine on cotton pledgets inserted along septum x 10 minutes
3. Local infiltration: lidocaine 1% with epinephrine along the inferior border of the hematoma (optional — topical usually sufficient)
4. Hemitransfixion incision at the most dependent point of the hematoma using #15 blade
5. Suction blood and clot
6. Irrigate cavity with sterile saline
7. Send aspirate for culture (aerobic + anaerobic) if abscess suspected

**Prevent Reaccumulation (critical step):**
- Anterior nasal packing: Merocel or ribbon gauze coated with antibiotic ointment, bilateral if bilateral hematoma
- OR quilting sutures: 4-0 chromic gut mattress sutures through both sides of the septum to obliterate dead space (preferred by ENT, may be performed in ED if skilled)
- Silastic septal splints — secured with through-and-through suture

**Packing Duration:**
- 24-48 hours, then re-examine
- If no reaccumulation, remove packing
- If reaccumulation, repeat drainage and re-pack

**Antibiotics (prophylactic — prevent septal abscess):**
- Amoxicillin-clavulanate 875/125 mg PO BID x 7 days
- Penicillin allergy: cephalexin 500 mg PO QID x 7 days (if non-anaphylactic PCN allergy) or clindamycin 300 mg PO TID x 7 days
- If septal abscess: ampicillin-sulbactam 3 g IV q6h + ENT consultation for operative drainage

**Analgesics:**
- Acetaminophen 500-1000 mg PO q6h + ibuprofen 400-600 mg PO q6h (alternating)
- Avoid aspirin (increases bleeding risk)

## Disposition

**Discharge (after successful drainage):**
- Hematoma drained, packing in place, no signs of abscess
- Antibiotics prescribed
- Follow-up with ENT within 24-48 hours for packing removal and re-examination
- Return precautions: fever, increasing pain, purulent nasal discharge, recurrent nasal obstruction after packing removal

**Admit:**
- Septal abscess (requires IV antibiotics and operative drainage under general anesthesia)
- Large bilateral hematoma with significant septal destruction
- Coagulopathy requiring reversal
- Associated midface fractures requiring operative fixation
- Pediatric patients where compliance with follow-up is uncertain and septal abscess risk is high

**ENT Referral (all patients):**
- All patients with septal hematoma require ENT follow-up regardless of disposition
- Late complications (septal perforation, saddle nose deformity) may require reconstructive surgery

## Pitfalls

1. **Not examining the nasal septum after nasal trauma.** This is the most common cause of missed septal hematoma. Every patient with nasal trauma — regardless of severity — requires anterior rhinoscopy with a nasal speculum. A penlight alone is insufficient. If the septum appears boggy or swollen, palpate with a cotton-tipped applicator.

2. **Mistaking septal hematoma for turbinate engorgement or mucosal edema.** Turbinate swelling is on the lateral nasal wall; septal hematoma is midline on the septum. Apply topical oxymetazoline — turbinates shrink, septal hematoma does not. Palpate: hematoma is fluctuant; edematous mucosa is not.

3. **Deferring drainage to outpatient ENT.** Septal cartilage has no intrinsic blood supply — it depends entirely on the overlying perichondrium. A hematoma separating perichondrium from cartilage causes avascular necrosis within 48-72 hours. Drainage is an emergency, not an elective procedure. If ENT is not available, the emergency physician must drain the hematoma.

4. **Failing to prevent reaccumulation after drainage.** Drainage without packing or quilting sutures leads to reaccumulation in 10-15% of cases. Always pack bilaterally or place quilting sutures after drainage. Re-examine at 24-48 hours.

5. **Missing a bilateral hematoma.** Blood can dissect across the cartilage from one side to the other. Always examine both sides of the septum. Bilateral hematomas are more common in children.

6. **Not considering septal abscess in late presentations.** A patient presenting > 72 hours after nasal trauma with fever, purulent nasal discharge, and a fluctuant septal mass likely has a septal abscess — not a simple hematoma. Aspirate: blood = hematoma; pus = abscess. Abscess requires IV antibiotics and operative drainage.

7. **Focusing on the nasal fracture while ignoring the septal hematoma.** Nasal fracture reduction can be deferred 5-7 days for swelling resolution. Septal hematoma drainage cannot wait. The hematoma is the urgent finding — treat it first.

8. **Not prescribing prophylactic antibiotics after drainage.** The drained hematoma cavity is a potential nidus for infection. Staphylococcus aureus and group A Streptococcus are the most common organisms in septal abscess. Prophylactic oral antibiotics for 7 days reduce this risk.
