# Basilar Skull Fracture

## Recognition

**Mechanism:** High-energy blunt trauma to head — motor vehicle collision, fall from height, assault. Temporal bone (75%), anterior fossa (25%), rarely posterior fossa.

**Clinical signs:**
- Raccoon eyes (periorbital ecchymosis) — anterior fossa fracture
- Battle sign (mastoid ecchymosis) — petrous temporal bone fracture; may take 12-72 hours to develop
- Hemotympanum — blood behind tympanic membrane
- CSF otorrhea (ear) or rhinorrhea (nose) — dural tear with CSF leak
- Cranial nerve palsies: CN VII (facial nerve) and CN VIII (vestibulocochlear) most common with temporal bone fractures; CN I (anosmia) with anterior fossa fractures

**Key diagnostics:**
- Non-contrast CT head with thin-cut temporal bone windows (1 mm cuts through skull base)
- CT angiography if fracture crosses carotid canal or dural venous sinuses
- Beta-2 transferrin assay on clear fluid to confirm CSF leak (gold standard; takes 24-48h)
- Halo sign on gauze (CSF forms ring around blood) — screening test, not definitive

## Critical Actions

| Action | Target |
|---|---|
| ATLS primary survey | Immediate |
| CT head + C-spine | < 30 minutes from arrival |
| Neurosurgery consult | < 1 hour if intracranial hemorrhage, depressed fracture, or CSF leak |
| CTA head/neck | If fracture crosses carotid canal or venous sinus |

1. **Secure airway** with cervical spine precautions — assume concurrent C-spine injury
2. **Avoid nasogastric tubes and nasotracheal intubation** — risk of intracranial passage through cribriform plate fracture; use orogastric tube and orotracheal intubation
3. **Reverse anticoagulation** immediately if on warfarin (vitamin K 10 mg IV + 4-factor PCC 25-50 units/kg IV) or DOACs (idarucizumab 5 g IV for dabigatran; andexanet alfa or 4-factor PCC for Xa inhibitors)
4. **Seizure prophylaxis** for severe TBI (GCS ≤ 8): levetiracetam 1000 mg IV x 1, then 500 mg IV q12h for 7 days
5. **Elevate head of bed 30 degrees** to reduce ICP and promote CSF leak cessation
6. **Do NOT pack ears or nose** to stop CSF leak — allow free drainage to prevent ascending meningitis

## Differential Diagnosis

- Isolated soft tissue contusion (no fracture on CT)
- Epidural hematoma — biconvex hyperdensity, often associated with temporal bone fracture and middle meningeal artery injury
- Subdural hematoma — crescent-shaped, may coexist
- Carotid artery dissection — fracture through carotid canal
- Cavernous sinus thrombosis — from fracture extension into sphenoid sinus
- Non-traumatic CSF leak — spontaneous intracranial hypotension

## Workup

- **CT head non-contrast** with thin-cut bone windows through skull base — primary diagnostic
- **CT cervical spine** — high association with C-spine injury
- **CTA head and neck** — if fracture crosses carotid canal, transverse sinus, or sigmoid sinus
- **Labs:** CBC, BMP, coagulation studies (PT/INR, PTT), type and screen
- **Beta-2 transferrin** on collected clear fluid (definitive CSF confirmation)
- **Audiometry** — arrange prior to discharge for temporal bone fractures
- **CT cisternography or intrathecal fluorescein** — if CSF leak source unclear (typically deferred to inpatient)

## Treatment

**CSF leak management:**
- Most CSF leaks resolve spontaneously within 7-10 days with conservative measures
- Elevate HOB 30 degrees, strict bed rest, stool softeners (docusate 100 mg PO BID), avoid straining/coughing/nose-blowing
- Lumbar drain placement if persistent leak > 7 days (neurosurgery)
- Surgical repair for leaks persisting > 2 weeks or recurrent meningitis

**Antibiotic prophylaxis:**
- Routine prophylactic antibiotics for CSF leak are NOT recommended (Cochrane review shows no benefit; may select resistant organisms)
- Treat meningitis aggressively if it develops: ceftriaxone 2 g IV q12h + vancomycin 15-20 mg/kg IV q8-12h

**Anticoagulation reversal:**
- Warfarin: vitamin K 10 mg IV slow infusion + 4-factor PCC 25-50 units/kg IV
- Dabigatran: idarucizumab 5 g IV (two 2.5 g boluses)
- Rivaroxaban/apixaban: 4-factor PCC 50 units/kg IV or andexanet alfa per protocol

**ICP management (if concurrent severe TBI):**
- Mannitol 1 g/kg IV bolus or hypertonic saline 3% 250 mL IV over 20 minutes
- Target PaCO2 35-40 mmHg (avoid prophylactic hyperventilation)

## Disposition

- **Admit:** All basilar skull fractures — minimum 24-hour observation
- **ICU admission:** GCS ≤ 12, active CSF leak, intracranial hemorrhage, anticoagulated patients
- **Neurosurgery consult:** Depressed fracture, intracranial hemorrhage, persistent CSF leak, CN palsy
- **ENT consult:** Temporal bone fracture with hearing loss or facial nerve palsy
- **Ophthalmology consult:** Orbital roof fracture with visual changes
- **Transfer:** To Level I trauma center if neurosurgery not available on site

## Pitfalls

1. **Placing a nasogastric tube in anterior fossa fracture.** Tube can pass through the cribriform plate into the brain. Always use orogastric route in suspected basilar skull fractures.

2. **Dismissing clear rhinorrhea as "runny nose" in a trauma patient.** CSF rhinorrhea indicates dural tear with meningitis risk. Test with beta-2 transferrin. Halo sign is a screening clue but not confirmatory.

3. **Missing temporal bone fracture because Battle sign has not yet appeared.** Mastoid ecchymosis may take 12-72 hours to develop. CT with thin-cut bone windows is required for early diagnosis.

4. **Failing to obtain CTA when fracture crosses the carotid canal.** Carotid dissection or pseudoaneurysm occurs in 25-50% of fractures through the carotid canal. Delayed stroke can follow.

5. **Prescribing prophylactic antibiotics for CSF leak.** Cochrane evidence shows no reduction in meningitis rates with prophylactic antibiotics. They promote resistant organisms.

6. **Packing the ear or nose to stop CSF drainage.** Obstruction prevents free drainage and creates a pathway for ascending meningitis. Allow gravity drainage and collect fluid for testing.
