# Le Fort Fractures

## Recognition

**Mechanism:** High-energy midface trauma — MVC, assault, fall from height. Force directed against midface.

**Classification:**
- **Le Fort I (horizontal/Guerin):** Fracture through maxilla above teeth, separating hard palate from upper midface. Fracture line through lateral nasal wall, maxillary sinus, pterygoid plates.
- **Le Fort II (pyramidal):** Fracture through nasal bridge, medial orbits, infraorbital rims, maxillary sinuses, pterygoid plates. "Floating maxilla" with pyramidal fragment.
- **Le Fort III (craniofacial dissociation):** Fracture through nasofrontal suture, orbits (medial and lateral walls), zygomatic arches, pterygoid plates. Complete separation of midface from skull base.

**Exam findings:**
- Midface mobility on bimanual palpation (grasp anterior maxillary teeth, stabilize forehead)
  - Le Fort I: mobile hard palate only
  - Le Fort II: mobile maxilla + nose (midface moves en bloc)
  - Le Fort III: entire face mobile relative to cranium ("dish face")
- Malocclusion, anterior open bite
- Massive facial edema, bilateral periorbital ecchymosis
- CSF rhinorrhea (especially Le Fort II/III)
- Epistaxis — can be life-threatening
- Le Fort III: elongated "donkey face" deformity

**Key diagnostics:**
- CT face with axial and coronal reformats — gold standard
- CT head for associated intracranial injury (50%+ with Le Fort III)
- CTA if concern for carotid or vertebral injury

## Critical Actions

| Action | Target |
|---|---|
| Airway control | Immediate — within minutes |
| Hemorrhage control | Immediate |
| CT face + head | < 30 minutes |
| Oral/maxillofacial surgery consult | < 1 hour |

1. **Secure the airway** — midface fractures compromise airway via hemorrhage, edema, posterior displacement of maxilla, and loss of tongue support. Early orotracheal intubation preferred. Surgical airway (cricothyrotomy) if oral intubation fails. **Avoid nasotracheal intubation** (risk of intracranial passage with concurrent skull base fracture).
2. **Control hemorrhage** — anterior and posterior nasal packing for epistaxis. If uncontrolled: Foley catheter posterior packing (14-16 Fr, inflate with 10-15 mL saline), anterior packing with ribbon gauze or commercial nasal tampon. Consider angioembolization by interventional radiology for refractory hemorrhage.
3. **Assess for associated injuries** — C-spine, intracranial hemorrhage, globe injury, cervical vascular injury.
4. **Administer TXA** if massive hemorrhage: tranexamic acid 1 g IV over 10 minutes, then 1 g IV over 8 hours.
5. **Antibiotics** if open fracture or contaminated wound: ampicillin-sulbactam 3 g IV q6h or clindamycin 900 mg IV q8h (if penicillin-allergic).

## Differential Diagnosis

- Isolated nasal fracture (no midface mobility)
- Zygomatic arch fracture (tripod/tetrapod fracture — isolated lateral cheek depression)
- Mandible fracture (lower jaw mobility, not midface)
- Naso-orbito-ethmoidal (NOE) fracture (telecanthus, medial canthal tendon disruption)
- Frontal sinus fracture (frontal bone depression, CSF rhinorrhea)
- Orbital blowout fracture (isolated orbital floor/wall without midface mobility)

## Workup

- **CT face** with thin cuts, axial and coronal reformats — defines fracture classification and surgical planning
- **CT head** — concurrent intracranial injury in 25-50% (especially Le Fort II/III)
- **CT C-spine** — high-energy mechanism mandates clearance
- **CTA head/neck** — if fracture extends to skull base or carotid canal
- **Labs:** CBC, BMP, coagulation studies, type and crossmatch (anticipate transfusion need)
- **Ophthalmology assessment** — visual acuity, extraocular movements, pupillary response, fundoscopy; entrapment and globe injury common

## Treatment

**Airway management:**
- Orotracheal intubation with video laryngoscopy preferred (blood, edema, distorted anatomy)
- Surgical airway (cricothyrotomy) if intubation fails
- Avoid blind nasotracheal intubation

**Hemorrhage control:**
- Sequential: direct pressure → anterior packing → posterior packing (Foley balloon) → angioembolization
- Massive transfusion protocol if hemodynamically unstable: 1:1:1 PRBC:FFP:platelets
- TXA 1 g IV over 10 minutes if within 3 hours of injury

**Medical management:**
- Tetanus prophylaxis: Tdap 0.5 mL IM if not current
- Antibiotics for open fractures: ampicillin-sulbactam 3 g IV q6h
- Pain: fentanyl 25-50 mcg IV q30min PRN (avoid agents that increase bleeding risk)
- Antiemetics: ondansetron 4 mg IV q6h PRN (vomiting + wired jaw is aspiration risk)

**Surgical management (OMFS/plastics):**
- Definitive ORIF typically delayed 5-10 days for edema resolution
- Exceptions requiring urgent OR: uncontrolled hemorrhage, airway compromise not managed by intubation, open fractures with contamination

## Disposition

- **Admit:** All Le Fort fractures require admission
- **ICU:** Le Fort III, concurrent TBI, hemodynamic instability, tracheostomy
- **OMFS/plastics consult:** All cases — mandatory for surgical planning
- **Neurosurgery consult:** Concurrent intracranial hemorrhage or CSF leak
- **Ophthalmology consult:** Any visual complaint, periorbital injury, suspected globe rupture
- **Transfer:** Level I trauma center if OMFS or neurosurgery not available

## Pitfalls

1. **Failing to assess midface mobility.** Massive edema obscures fractures on exam. Grasp the anterior teeth and attempt anteroposterior motion while stabilizing the forehead. This is the critical bedside maneuver.

2. **Attempting nasotracheal intubation or placing NGT with possible skull base fracture.** Le Fort II and III fractures frequently extend to skull base. Nasal instrumentation risks intracranial passage. Use oral route exclusively.

3. **Underestimating hemorrhage.** Le Fort fractures can cause life-threatening posterior epistaxis from internal maxillary artery branches. Anticipate need for massive transfusion protocol and have IR embolization available.

4. **Missing concurrent cervical spine injury.** High-energy mechanism causing Le Fort fracture has high association with C-spine injury. Maintain immobilization until cleared.

5. **Delayed ophthalmologic assessment.** Orbital involvement in Le Fort II/III can cause optic nerve compression, retrobulbar hemorrhage, or globe rupture. Irreversible vision loss within 90-120 minutes without decompression.

6. **Discharging Le Fort I as "just a maxillary fracture."** Even Le Fort I can develop progressive airway compromise from edema over 12-24 hours. All Le Fort fractures require admission with serial airway assessments.
